	\begin{abstract}
	Federated Learning (Federated Learning (FL) requires simultaneously addressing privacy, Byzantine robustness, and communication efficiency, forming a privacy--robustness--performance trilemma. This work empirically investigates the interactions among \ac{DP-SGD}, FLTrust aggregation, and Top-$k$ gradient sparsification. Using a modular experimental pipeline, we evaluate their isolated and combined effects on model accuracy, attack resilience, and communication overhead. Experiments were conducted on the MNIST dataset under \glspl{labelflippingattack}, systematically varying \glspl{privacybudget}, \glspl{sparsificationratio}, and local optimization schedules. Results indicate that combining \ac{DP-SGD} and FLTrust severely degrades performance during full local-epoch training. However, reducing \glspl{localsgdstep} substantially mitigates this degradation and reveals an unexpected dependence of model performance on the selected \gls{privacybudget}. Furthermore, Top-$k$ sparsification interacts largely additively with FLTrust, while exhibiting super-additive degradation when combined with \ac{DP-SGD}. Finally, analyzing the temporal evolution of FLTrust \glspl{trustscore} provides critical insights into the behavioral divergence of honest and \glspl{maliciousclient} throughout training.
	\end{abstract}

	\subsection{Background and Motivation}
	
	In the past ten years, \ac{FL} has emerged as a framework for achieving distributed training without the need for data centralization. This is of particular interest when processing sensitive data, such as in healthcare or mobile applications. Local training allows clients to keep their data on-device but opens new attack surfaces for model poisoning during training through the transmission of manipulated updates. Also, the transmitted gradients can still leak information about the used training data set. Additionally, gradient transmission at each round causes significant communication cost, especially for complex models.
	
	In an effort to fortify the resilience of models and safeguard client privacy, numerous methodologies have been implemented in recent years. 
	Robustness-enhancing methods are designed to detect malicious updates by assuming a significant deviation from the honest majority. 
	Privacy-preserving algorithms add a mask or calibrated noise to the local updates before transmitting them to the central server.
	
	\subsection{Problem Statement}
	
	Ensuring model robustness and client privacy while balancing communication cost and model accuracy has been identified as a significant challenge.
	Adding privacy-enhancing noise to individual updates reduces a malicious server's ability to extract information about local datasets, while simultaneously reducing model accuracy.
	Discarding unusual updates improves robustness against \glspl{byzantineadversary} but risks disregarding legitimate updates, particularly in settings where data is not \ac{IID}.
	Compression techniques that reduce transmission load limit the information transmitted to the server and thus reduce model accuracy.
	
	In 2023, Allouah et al.~\cite{allouah2023trilemma} proved Theorem~\ref{theorem:lowerBound}, stating that the combination of such methods yields a lower bound for the expected training error that exceeds the sum of the individual cost for robustness and privacy by a multiplicative term.
	\begin{theorem}
		Consider distributed mean estimation in $\mathbb{R}^d$ with $n$ users, of which an $\eta$-fraction may behave maliciously. Any algorithm, producing an estimate $\hat{\mu}$ of the true mean $\mu\in\mathbb{R}^d$ of inputs, and achieving $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-\gls{localdp} and robustness to $\eta$-corruption must incur an estimated error
		\begin{equation}
			\mathbb{E}\parallel \hat{\mu}-\mu\parallel _{2}^{2} = \tilde{\Omega}(\frac{\eta}{\varepsilon^{2}}+\frac{d}{\varepsilon^{2}n}+\eta G^{2}).
		\end{equation}
		\label{theorem:lowerBound}
	\end{theorem}
	
	A position paper by the same group~\cite{allouah2023balancing} highlights a gap between the theoretical lower bound and current
	implementations. 
	
	\subsection{Research Questions}
	
	The project is structured around three research questions:
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item \textbf{Isolation:} How do differential privacy, Byzantine-robust aggregation, and gradient compression individually affect model accuracy, robustness, and computational efficiency?
		\item \textbf{Combination:} How do pairwise and full combinations of the three
		mechanisms interact? Do the costs compound additively, sub-additively, or
		super-additively?
		\item \textbf{Trust and Privacy:} How do the \glspl{trustscore} assigned by FLTrust evolve throughout training, and what do they reveal about the interaction between privacy, robustness, and model performance?
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\subsection{Contributions}
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item An open-source, end-to-end federated learning framework that integrates \ac{DP-SGD}, FLTrust, and Top-$k$ sparsification in a modular architecture, facilitating reproducible experiments and future research on their interaction.
		
		\item An empirical evaluation of the isolated and combined effects of \ac{DP-SGD}, FLTrust, and Top-$k$ sparsification on model performance, robustness, and efficiency in federated learning.
		
		\item Experimental evidence that the interaction between \ac{DP-SGD} and FLTrust strongly depends on the local optimization schedule, showing that reducing the number of \glspl{localsgdstep} can substantially mitigate the performance degradation observed when combining both mechanisms.
		
		\item An analysis of the temporal evolution of FLTrust \glspl{trustscore}, providing insights into the behavior of honest and \glspl{maliciousclient} throughout training and identifying this aspect as an underexplored area for future research.
		
		\item A quantitative comparison of the communication, computational, and robustness trade-offs introduced by Top-$k$ sparsification, \ac{DP-SGD}, and FLTrust, both individually and in combination.
		
		\item A visualization framework that maps privacy, robustness, and efficiency onto a common three-axis representation to facilitate the analysis of the privacy--robustness--performance trilemma.
	\end{itemize}

	\section{Background}
	\label{sec:background}
	% --------------------------------------------------
	This section summarizes the theoretical background of \ac{FL}, the trilemma axes and formalizes the Privacy-Robustness-Performance trilemma.
	\subsection{Federated Learning}
	
	Federated Learning is a machine learning framework comprising a central server and multiple distributed clients. Each client maintains a
	local dataset to compute updates for the \gls{globalmodel}. The central server orchestrates the training process by distributing the current
	\gls{globalmodel}, collecting client updates, and aggregating them to produce a new \gls{globalmodel} update. This update is then distributed to clients to initiate the next training round. 
	
	The most widely used aggregation method is \gls{fedavg}~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}, which computes
	the global update as a weighted average of the client-local model updates.
	
	\subsection{Privacy: \ac{DP}}
	This section introduces the concepts of local and \gls{centraldp} and describes the implementation of \gls{localdp} in \ac{DP-SGD}.
	
	\subsubsection{Definition and Composition}

	\ac{DP} forms a good standard for privacy guarantees for algorithms used in data aggregation. Its degree of protection is defined by the \gls{privacybudget} $\varepsilon$ and the failure probability $\delta$. We use the definition given by Abadi et al.~\cite{abadi2016deep}.
	
\begin{definition}
	A randomized mechanism $M\colon D\rightarrow R$ with domain $D$ and range $R$ 
	satisfies $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-differential privacy if for any two adjacent inputs $d,d'\in D$ and for any subset of outputs $S\subseteq R$ it holds that
	\[
	\Pr[M(D)\in S]\leq e^{\varepsilon}\Pr[M(D')\in S]+\delta.
	\]
\end{definition}

	The \gls{privacybudget} $\varepsilon$ denotes the trade-off between utility and privacy, bounding the tolerated noise addition. Small values ($\varepsilon < 1$) provide the strongest privacy guarantees while larger values ($\varepsilon >8$) allow for more accurate training.
	
	The failure probability $\delta$, introduced by Dwork et al.~\cite{privacy_delta}, denotes the probability that the plain $\varepsilon$-\ac{DP} is broken and should be chosen smaller than the inverse size of the local dataset.
	
	\paragraph{Central vs.\ \Gls{localdp} in FL.}
	
	In central DP, the server adds noise after aggregating the client updates. This setting assumes a trusted aggregator while protecting the aggregated updates before they are shared with third parties.
	
	In the considered setting, client updates must be protected against inference attacks by the aggregator or adversaries eavesdropping on the client--server communication, requiring \gls{localdp}. To achieve this, each client adds noise to its update before transmission. Since the noise added by individual clients accumulates during aggregation, \gls{localdp} requires a higher effective noise magnitude and therefore incurs a greater loss in accuracy.
	
	\paragraph{\ac{DP-SGD}.}
	
	\ac{DP-SGD} is a modification to the standard \ac{SGD} algorithm introduced by Abadi et al.~\cite{abadi2016deep} in 2016 to integrate \gls{localdp} into deep learning frameworks. It enhances \ac{SGD} by clipping the computed gradient to a fixed norm and adding calibrated (Gaussian) noise before performing an update step. The algorithm pseudocode as proposed by Abadi et al. is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dpsgdpseudocode}.
	
	\begin{figure}
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{img/DP_SGD_pseudocode}
		\caption{\ac{DP-SGD} pseudocode as published by Abadi et al.~\cite{abadi2016deep}.}
		\label{fig:dpsgdpseudocode}
	\end{figure}
	
	
	\subsection{Robustness: Byzantine-Tolerant Aggregation}
	This section introduces the Byzantine fault model and presents two aggregation schemes designed to mitigate the effects of \glspl{byzantineadversary}.
	
	\paragraph{Byzantine Fault Model.}
	Byzantine Fault Tolerance is a property of a distributed system that allows for the system to stay operational while some components fail or act maliciously.
	
	A $\eta$-fraction \gls{byzantineadversary} means that a fraction of $\eta$ of the clients are acting maliciously. In the conducted experiments, malicious behavior means communicating manipulated gradients to poison the central model, e.g. insert a backdoor, degrade the general performance or enable \glspl{labelflippingattack}.
	
	\paragraph{FLTrust.}
	
	FLTrust~\cite{cao2021fltrust} was introduced by Cao et al. in 2020 and mitigates malicious updates through \glspl{trustscore}. These are obtained by calculating the cosine similarity of the local update to an \gls{updateanchor} computed on a small \gls{rootdataset} held by the server. The method relies on the assumption that the server has access to a dataset representing the general data distribution across all clients.
	
	\subsection{Communication Efficiency}
	Another practical limitation especially in mobile applications is the available bandwidth to transmit local updates to the aggregator. We therefore include a method to communicate sparse instead of dense updates.
	
	\paragraph{Top-$k$ Gradient Sparsification.}
	
	Top-$k$ gradient sparsification~\cite{aji2017topk} reduces communication during gradient transmission by retaining only the $k$ largest gradient components, where $k$ is a fixed fraction of the model dimension $d$.
	
	Discarding the remaining gradient components inevitably results in information loss, leading to reduced model performance. The extent of this degradation depends on how the optimization information is distributed across the gradient, with models relying on a small number of dominant components generally being less affected than those requiring information from many dimensions.
	
	\subsection{The Privacy--Robustness--Performance Trilemma}
	This section first presents the theoretical lower bound of the trilemma and then discusses its implications for empirical evaluation, motivating efficiency as a fundamental third axis.
		
	\paragraph{Theoretical Lower Bound.}
	
	Allouah et al.~\cite{allouah2023trilemma} proved that the mean training error for any algorithm simultaneously guaranteeing $\epsilon$-local DP and robustness against an $\eta$-fraction \gls{byzantineadversary} has a fixed lower bound as mentioned in Theorem~\ref{eq:trilemma_formula}.
	This lower bound establishes that the costs of privacy and Byzantine robustness are fundamentally coupled. In particular, the cross-term $\eta/\epsilon^2$ shows that these costs interact multiplicatively rather than additively.
	
	\paragraph{Efficiency Axis Extension.}
	
	The same authors proposed the aggregation schemes \ac{SMEA} and \ac{CAF}, which match this theoretical lower bound. However, both methods incur a runtime complexity that scales cubically with the model dimension, rendering them impractical for large-scale models. Furthermore, the runtime of SMEA grows exponentially with the number of tolerated \glspl{byzantineadversary}, making it infeasible in realistic federated learning settings.
	
	The term $\frac{d}{\varepsilon^2 n}$ in Equation~\ref{lowerBoundTheory} further relates the model dimension $d$ to the communication cost and achievable estimation error. Consequently, maintaining accuracy for high-dimensional models requires either relaxing privacy guarantees or integrating a larger number of participating clients.
	
	
	\paragraph{Empirical Evidence of Compounding.}
	
	In 2021, Guerraoui et al. empirically investigated the question \textit{"Differential Privacy and Byzantine Resilience in SGD: Do They Add Up?"}~\cite{guerraoui2021differentialprivacybyzantineresilience}. They showed that a naive combination of privacy- and robustness-enhancing methods, namely \ac{DP-SGD} and Minimum-Diameter Averaging (MDA)~\cite{elmhamdi2020genuinelydistributedbyzantinemachine}, leads to unfavorable scaling with the model dimensionality and super-additive performance degradation. These findings support the theoretical results of Allouah et al. and reinforce the need to treat efficiency as a fundamental third axis in future experimental studies.

	\section{Threat Model and Metrics}
	\label{sec:threat}
	% --------------------------------------------------
	This section maps out the assumed threat model and introduces the metrics used to evaluate the three axes.
	
	\subsection{Threat Model}
	
	We assume an honest-but-curious central server that adheres to protocol specifications but may attempt to infer private information from transmitted updates, motivating the use of differential privacy (DP) to safeguard client data. The adversary is characterized by a fraction $\eta$ of \glspl{maliciousclient}, capable of arbitrarily manipulating gradients (e.g., through \glspl{labelflippingattack}, as formalized in Fang et al.~\cite{fang2020poisoning}). These clients aim to corrupt the \gls{globalmodel} by injecting adversarial updates. Crucially, we assume no collusion between Byzantine clients and the server, restricting adversarial behavior to local manipulations of gradients. This assumption simplifies the analysis while maintaining practical relevance for decentralized learning systems.
	
	\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
	\label{sec:metrics}
	
	As suggested by Allouah et al.~\cite{allouah2023balancing}, every configuration is evaluated simultaneously across all three trilemma axes:
	
	\paragraph{Privacy.}
	
	The privacy objective is quantified by the assigned $(\epsilon,\delta)$-differential privacy (DP) budget.
	
	\paragraph{Robustness.}
	
	Robustness is evaluated by the \gls{accuracygap} $\Delta_{acc}$ relative to a clean \gls{fedavg} baseline. The \gls{accuracygap} captures performance degradation caused by adversarial clients, providing a measure of resilience to Byzantine behavior.
	
	\paragraph{Efficiency.}

	The efficiency metric includes communication cost (bytes sent per client per round), and wall-clock training time. These metrics collectively assess the trade-off between resource efficiency, and computational feasibility in decentralized learning systems.
	
	\subsection{Trilemma Visualization}
	
	The values of the three trilemma axes are computed as follows, each yielding a value in the range $[0,1]$, where 0 denotes no effect and 1 the maximum attainable effect:
	\begin{align*}
		&\text{Privacy} = \max\left(0,\frac{\varepsilon_{\max}-\varepsilon}{\varepsilon_{\max}}\right)\\
		&\text{Robustness} = 1-\Delta_{acc}\\
		&\text{Efficiency} = \alpha\times\frac{t_{base}}{t}+(1-\alpha)\times\frac{b_{base}-b}{b_{base}}
		\label{eq:trilemma_formula}
	\end{align*}
	
	Here, $\varepsilon$ denotes the \gls{privacybudget}, $\Delta_{acc}$ the \gls{accuracygap} induced by the Byzantine attack, $t$ the wall-clock training time per client and \gls{communicationround}, and $b$ the number of bytes transmitted per client and \gls{communicationround}. The reference values $t_{base}$ and $b_{base}$ correspond to the wall-clock training time and transmitted bytes of the baseline configuration, respectively.
	
	The weighting parameter $\alpha$ determines the relative contribution of computation time and communication cost to the efficiency axis and can be adjusted to match the requirements of a target deployment. Unless stated otherwise, $\alpha=0.5$ is used to assign equal weight to both components.

	\section{Methods}
	\label{sec:method}
	% --------------------------------------------------
	This section briefly describes the implementation of the selected methods for each axis.
	\subsection{Baseline: \gls{fedavg}}
	
	\gls{fedavg}, introduced by McMahan et al. in 2017~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}, is a widely used aggregation strategy in \ac{FL} designed to handle non-\ac{IID} data efficiently. It computes a weighted average of \gls{localmodel} updates, with weights determined by the size of each client’s local dataset. The paper establishes baselines on standard datasets such as MNIST and CIFAR-10, making \gls{fedavg} a foundational reference for evaluating \ac{FL} methods. Its simplicity and effectiveness in early \ac{FL} research have cemented its role as a standard baseline for comparative studies.
	
	\subsection{Privacy Mechanism: \ac{DP-SGD}}
	
	\ac{DP-SGD} is implemented according to the algorithm described in Figure~\ref{fig:dpsgdpseudocode}. Before each optimization step, the $\ell_2$ norm of each per-sample gradient is clipped to the threshold $C$. The clipped gradients are averaged, and Gaussian noise sampled from $\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 C^2 I)$ is added before updating the model parameters, where $\sigma$ denotes the noise multiplier. The experiments evaluate \glspl{privacybudget} of $\epsilon \in {1,5,10}$ while fixing $\delta = 10^{-5}$.
	
	
	\subsection{Robustness Mechanism: FLTrust}
	
	The \gls{rootdataset} for the central server was obtained by randomly sampling a parametrized number of data points, uniformly distributed over all classes, from the training set.
	
	The \gls{trustscore} for weighting the local contributions is calculated as the cosine similarity to the update vector that was computed from the root data set as depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:fltrustupdate}.
	\begin{figure*}[h!]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{img/fltrust_update}
		\caption{Global aggregation rule for FLTrust as introduced by Cao et al.~\cite{cao2021fltrust}.}
		\label{fig:fltrustupdate}
	\end{figure*}
	
	\subsection{Efficiency Mechanism: Top-$k$ Sparsification}
	
	Top-$k$ sparsification is implemented as a custom client-side wrapper that retains only the $k\%$ gradient components with the largest magnitude, where $k \in {1,10,50}$, and transmits their corresponding indices alongside the retained values. Consequently, the number of transmitted bytes is not reduced exactly to $k\%$ of the original update size because the component indices must also be communicated. Nevertheless, a substantial reduction in communication volume is expected, particularly for small values of $k$.

	
	
	\subsection{Mechanism Interaction Effects}
	\label{sec:interaction}
	In this section we outline the expected compounding effects.
	
	\paragraph{Performance.}
	We hypothesize that the clipping and calibrated noise introduced by \ac{DP-SGD} impair the assignment of meaningful \glspl{trustscore} during FLTrust aggregation, thereby slowing convergence and reducing the final model accuracy beyond the individual cost of \ac{DP-SGD}. Furthermore,we expect  \ac{DP}-perturbed updates to be more sensitive to Top-$k$ sparsification, as the added noise may increase the magnitude of gradient components that would otherwise be discarded. Consequently, we expect combinations of multiple mechanisms to incur a super-additive loss in accuracy.
	
	\paragraph{Efficiency.}
	We expect the noise addition required for \ac{DP-SGD} to introduce a noticeable computational overhead on the client side, while FLTrust incurs a slight overhead on the server side. In contrast, Top-$k$ sparsification is not expected to reduce the overall training time in our experimental setup, as all clients are simulated on a single machine and communication latency is therefore negligible. However, the number of bytes transmitted per training round is expected to decrease proportionally to the \gls{sparsificationratio}.

	\subsection{Dataset}

	\paragraph{MNIST.} The MNIST dataset consists of 70,000 grayscale images of handwritten digits with a resolution of 28 × 28 pixels, each labelled with the corresponding digit in the range [0,9]. It is divided into a training set of 60,000 samples and a test set of 10,000 samples. Owing to its simplicity, the dataset is widely used to benchmark image classification models and verify their functionality before extending them to more complex datasets.
	
	\subsection{Learning and attack settings}

	For a training setup with $n$ clients, the dataset is partitioned into $n+1$ disjoint subsets: one uniformly sampled \gls{rootdataset} for FLTrust and $n$ randomly sampled client datasets. We assume an \ac{IID} data distribution across all clients and therefore do not introduce artificial label imbalances. The resulting label distribution for the MNIST experiments is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:labeldistr}.
	
	The \gls{labelflippingattack} is implemented by swapping the labels of the digits "3" and "7" in the MNIST dataset and the classes "cats" and "dogs" in the CIFAR-10 dataset for the \glspl{maliciousclient}. The resulting gradients are subsequently scaled by the \gls{attackscale} parameter before being transmitted to the central server to amplify the attack.

	
	\subsection{Parameters}

	\paragraph{Static.} The following parameters are constant across all experiment runs.

	\begin{itemize}
		\item The client learning rate is fixed at 0.01.
		\item The number of gradient descent steps taken by each client per round is set to 10, based on the analysis in Section~\ref{batches_per_round_trustscores}.
		\item The initial seed for random processes is fixed to 42 to ensure comparability across runs. Stochastic components required to vary between rounds, like training set shuffle or DP noise generation, additionally take the round number as input.
		\item The attack type is fixed to flip the labels of digits 3 and 7 on the MNIST dataset.
		\item \ac{DP} failure probability $\delta$ is fixed to 1e-5.
		\item The \gls{rootdataset} size for FLTrust is fixed to 2000 samples. This is very large compared to the size of 100 samples proposed by the authors.
	\end{itemize}

		\item \Gls{attackscale} for \gls{labelflippingattack}
		\item \ac{DP} budget $\varepsilon \in [1;5;10]$
		\item Top-$k$ sparsification threshold $k \in [1\%;10\%;50\%]$

	\section{Results}
	\label{sec:results}
	% --------------------------------------------------
	This section summarizes the experimental results. The final round accuracy over all configurations is reported in Figure~\ref{fig:bars_acc_exp1}.
	\subsection{Baseline Reproduction (RQ1 -- Sanity Check)}
	
	Training a clean \gls{fedavg} model using only \glspl{honestclient} on the MNIST dataset yielded a final accuracy of 98\%, slightly below the 99\% reported in the original \gls{fedavg} paper~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}.
	
	The original FLTrust paper reported an accuracy of 99.6\% on the MNIST dataset for both clean \gls{fedavg} training and training under attack when FLTrust was enabled. In our experiments, enabling FLTrust achieved a final accuracy of 98\%, matching the performance of the clean \gls{fedavg} baseline. Although the absolute accuracy is lower than that reported by the original authors, our results likewise indicate that FLTrust effectively mitigates the \gls{labelflippingattack} under the considered experimental setting.

	
	\subsection{Isolation: Individual Mechanism Costs (RQ1)}
	
	This section analyzes the cost and effects of the isolated mechanisms compared to the baseline.

	\subsubsection{\ac{DP-SGD} Cost}
	
	
	\begin{figure*}
		\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{img/MNIST_07-12/duration_per_config.png}

	\end{figure*}
	
	\paragraph{Training time.}
	The replacement of pure \ac{SGD} with \ac{DP-SGD} on the clients results in an increase in training time. This effect is stronger for stricter \glspl{privacybudget} as depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:duration_by_client_number}.
	
	\paragraph{Final accuracy.}
	Regarding the model performance, all conducted experiments report an accuracy loss of $\approx 0.2$ compared to the baseline (see Figures~\ref{fig:bars_acc_exp1} and~\ref{fig:bars_accexp2}).
	Contrary to our expectations, the assigned \gls{privacybudget} does not have a major influence on the accuracy besides the general degradation observed when applying \ac{DP-SGD}: We achieve similar performance for all $\varepsilon \in [1;5;10;25]$. Figure~\ref{fig:DP_acc} shows a slight variation in the early rounds but overall a similar evolution for all values of $\varepsilon$.
	
	\subsubsection{FLTrust}

	\paragraph{Effect.}
	Enabling FLTrust improves model accuracy and mitigates the effects of the \gls{labelflippingattack}, as shown in Figures~\ref{fig:confusion_matrix_base} and~\ref{fig:confusion_matrix_fltrust}. In the baseline model, the targeted digits 3 and 7 are consistently misclassified as each other, whereas enabling FLTrust largely restores correct predictions. The improvement becomes more pronounced with increasing \glspl{byzantinefraction} and \glspl{attackscale}. The results shown were obtained with a \gls{byzantinefraction} of $\eta = 0.4$ and an \gls{attackscale} of 2.0.
	
	A lower \gls{byzantinefraction} results in a better performance of the unprotected settings and therefore lowers the observable effect of FLTrust.
	
	\paragraph{Cost.}
	The replacement of \gls{fedavg} aggregation with FLTrust did not yield a noticeable impact on the wall clock training time (compare Figure~\ref{fig:duration_by_client_number}).

	\subsubsection{Top-$k$ Sparsification}

	\paragraph{Effect.}
	Applying Top-$k$ sparsification yields the expected savings in bandwidth, modeled here by measuring the bytes transferred per client per round. The results are visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:bytes_per_round}, confirming a reduction of $(100-2k)\%$. The factor of two comes from the necessity to transfer also the indices of the transferred bytes, rendering all $k\ge50$ impractical for saving bandwidth.
	
	\begin{figure}

		\caption{KB transferred per client per round for settings with different Top-$k$ sparsification thresholds.}
		\label{fig:bytes_per_round}
	\end{figure}
	
	\paragraph{Cost.}
	We do not observe a significant decline in performance when applying Top-$k$ sparsification to the MNIST training process. Cutting down to transferring only the top 1\% of the gradient yields a slight loss in accuracy, but not a noticeable effect.
	
	One possible explanation for the minimal effect of the sparsification is that MNIST is a relatively simple dataset for which the classification depends on a very limited number of features.
	
	\subsection{Combination: Pairwise and Full Interactions (RQ2)}
	
	This section reports the combinatory effects observed when pairing multiple mechanisms.
	The full results are available in Table~\ref{tab:accuracy-summary}.
	\subsubsection{\ac{DP} + Robustness}
	
	\paragraph{Cost.}
	Combining \ac{DP-SGD} with FLTrust results in a substantial decline in model accuracy when training full epochs per round, reducing performance to only slightly above random guessing. Figure~\ref{fig:confusion_DP+FLTRUST} shows the confusion matrix of the final model, revealing that it predicts only two classes.
	
	These results are consistent with the super-additive interaction between privacy and Byzantine robustness derived by Allouah et al. Intuitively, the noise introduced for differential privacy causes client updates to deviate from the anchor update computed on the \gls{rootdataset}, resulting in uniformly low \glspl{trustscore}. This behavior is reflected in the second chart of Figure~\ref{fig:trustscores_over_rounds}.
	
	When reducing the local training steps to 10 batches per round (final accuracies available in Figure~\ref{fig:bars_accexp2}), the decline is surprisingly soft, surpassing the results of \ac{DP} alone and showcasing the same \gls{accuracygap} against the FLTrust-only results as the \ac{DP}-only config did against the base configuration
	Contrary to our expectations, for the combination of FLTrust and \ac{DP}, stricter privacy guarantees ($\varepsilon=1$) led to even better accuracy than more relaxed ones ($\varepsilon=10$), resulting in \glspl{accuracygap} down to $\Delta_{acc}=0.05$ compared to the honest \gls{fedavg} setting.
	
	\paragraph{Influence of the $\varepsilon$ value.}
	Although model performance varies slightly with $\varepsilon$, even the relaxed setting of $\varepsilon = 25$ does not achieve an accuracy above 0.2. The most pronounced effect is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:DP+FLTRUST_acc}, which illustrates the accuracy throughout training. While the configuration with $\varepsilon = 10$ reaches a peak accuracy of approximately 0.2 after 15 training rounds, the configuration with $\varepsilon = 1$ never exceeds an accuracy of 0.12, indicating substantially slower convergence.


	
	\subsubsection{\ac{DP} + Compression}
	
	Applying Top-$k$ sparsification to updates perturbed by \ac{DP} noise significantly affects model performance, an effect that is not observed in the Top-$k$-only configuration. While $k=50\%$ does not introduce additional degradation beyond that caused by \ac{DP-SGD} alone, smaller values of $k$ substantially reduce the final model accuracy, with $k=1\%$ reaching a peak accuracy of only 0.2 when training full epochs and 0.27 when reducing that to 10 SGD steps per round.
	
	As in the \ac{DP}-only configuration, varying the \gls{privacybudget} $\varepsilon$ has no noticeable impact on model accuracy.
	
	\subsubsection{Robustness + Compression}
	
	Combining FLTrust with Top-$k$ sparsification largely preserves the individual effects of both methods. Transmitting only 1\% of the gradient results in the same slight reduction in accuracy observed in the Top-$k$-only configuration, while FLTrust provides a comparable improvement in robustness to that achieved with dense updates. Figure~\ref{fig:trustscores_over_rounds} shows that the \glspl{trustscore} exhibit a similar evolution to the dense-update setting.
	
	\subsubsection{Full Combination (\ac{DP} + Robustness + Compression)}
	
	For full local-epoch training, combining all three methods reduces model performance to a level comparable to that of the \ac{DP}+FLTrust configuration. The final accuracy ranges between 0.12 and 0.18, only marginally exceeding random guessing, and the resulting confusion matrix closely resembles that of the \ac{DP}+FLTrust setting.
	
	Reducing the local training to 10 steps per \gls{communicationround}, however, produces markedly different results. The performance degradation previously observed for Top-1\% sparsification in the \ac{DP}+Top-$k$ configuration no longer occurs when FLTrust is added. Furthermore, the trend of stronger privacy guarantees yielding higher accuracy persists across all evaluated values of $k$. For a configuration with 60 clients, an accuracy of 88\% is achieved with a \gls{privacybudget} of $\varepsilon = 1$ while transmitting only 1\% of the locally computed gradient components to the server.
	
	\subsection{Trust and Privacy: FLTrust scores (RQ3)}
	This section reports the findings related to the \glspl{trustscore} computed during the FLTrust aggregation.
	
	\paragraph{Development of \glspl{trustscore}.}
	Figure~\ref{fig:trustscores_over_rounds} illustrates the evolution of the \glspl{trustscore} throughout training. The first chart, corresponding to the isolated FLTrust configuration, shows a clear separation between honest and \glspl{maliciousclient}. \Glspl{honestclient} start with \glspl{trustscore} of approximately 0.9, which gradually decrease after roughly 15 rounds before stabilizing around 0.3. \Glspl{maliciousclient} start near 0.7, exhibit a brief increase, and then decline sharply to 0 after approximately 25 training rounds.
	
	One possible explanation for the gradual decline in the \glspl{trustscore} of \glspl{honestclient} is that differences between the local training datasets become increasingly influential once the model has learned the general structure of the data. Consequently, honest updates remain broadly aligned with the \gls{rootdataset} but become progressively more diverse, whereas the poisoned and scaled updates of \glspl{maliciousclient} deviate from the server \gls{updateanchor} and are therefore assigned \glspl{trustscore} close to zero.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% --------------------------------------------------

	In this section, we compare the experimental results with the hypotheses formulated in Section~\ref{sec:interaction}.
	
	\paragraph{Influence of the Optimization Schedule on the Interaction of Differential Privacy and FLTrust.}
	Contrary to our hypothesis, the combination of \ac{DP-SGD} and FLTrust did not generally result in a super-additive decline in performance, but only showed the expected degradation when training in full epochs. For this initial setting, we observed the expected behavior: The \glspl{trustscore} of both honest and \glspl{maliciousclient} collapse during the early stages of training, preventing further convergence and limiting the model accuracy to little more than random guessing.
	
	One possible explanation is that, during full \glspl{localepoch}, the client models drift apart, causing the updates to naturally become more dissimilar. The addition of privacy-preserving noise further amplifies this drift, resulting in the collapse of the \glspl{trustscore}. With fewer \glspl{localsgdstep}, client drift remains limited, preserving a higher degree of similarity between \gls{honestclient} updates and the FLTrust anchor update. Consequently, the additional perturbation introduced by differential privacy is no longer sufficient to destroy the \gls{trustscore} assignment.
	
	These findings suggest that the optimization schedule constitutes an important moderating factor in the interaction between differential privacy and Byzantine robustness. While the theoretical lower bound remains valid, the practical severity of the interaction appears to depend strongly on the amount of local optimization performed between \glspl{communicationround}.
	
	\paragraph{Interaction of \ac{DP} and FLTrust.}
	Enabling FLTrust led to an inversion of the expected decline in performance for stronger privacy guarantees. We did not find a solid explanation for this phenomenon during our experiments and suggest that future work investigate this behavior further. This observation indicates that the interaction between differential privacy and Byzantine robustness may be more complex than suggested by the theoretical lower bound alone.
	
	\paragraph{Interaction of Sparsification and \ac{DP}.}
	As expected, applying aggressive sparsification to gradients perturbed by privacy-preserving noise degrades performance super-additively because the transmitted dimensions are no longer necessarily those carrying the most useful information, but may instead include components whose magnitudes were increased by the added noise.
	
	\paragraph{Interaction of FLTrust and Top-$k$ Sparsification.}
	Sparsifying the gradients prior to transmission did not have a noticeable impact on either the final model accuracy or the \glspl{trustscore} computed during aggregation. A possible explanation is that cosine similarity is largely insensitive to changes in vector magnitude. Since Top-$k$ sparsification preserves the dominant gradient components, the direction of the client updates remains sufficiently similar for FLTrust to assign meaningful \glspl{trustscore}.
	
	\paragraph{Evolution of Trust Scores.}
	The temporal behavior of the \glspl{trustscore} of honest and \glspl{maliciousclient} has received little attention in the literature. One possible explanation for the observed dynamics is that, during the early stages of training, the model learns the general structure of the data, resulting in relatively similar client updates. Once the model has captured these global patterns, the honest updates increasingly reflect the characteristics of the individual local datasets and gradually drift apart, causing their \glspl{trustscore} to decrease. At the same time, the influence of the label-flipping attack becomes increasingly dominant in the malicious updates, leading to a collapse of their \glspl{trustscore}.


	
	% --------------------------------------------------
	\section{Limitations and Future Work}
	\label{sec:limitations}
	% --------------------------------------------------
	
	\subsection{Limitations}
	The experiments were conducted under several simplifying assumptions to ensure a manageable experimental setup while establishing a baseline for future research.
	
	\paragraph{Simple models and datasets.}
	The selected datasets and models facilitate comparison with existing work but may not adequately represent the data characteristics and model architectures encountered in practical \ac{FL} applications, such as medical, financial, or transformer-based systems.
	
	\paragraph{Limited method coverage.}
	Only a single mechanism and, where applicable, a single attack type were evaluated for each axis of the trilemma. Furthermore, the baseline for the membership inference attack success rate was approximated using a shadow model, and the evaluated \glspl{privacybudget} were limited to a small $\varepsilon$-grid to balance computational feasibility with analytical rigor.
	
	\paragraph{FLTrust \gls{rootdataset} size.}
	The comparatively large \gls{rootdataset} may overestimate the effectiveness of the FLTrust defense mechanism. The experiments should therefore be extended to investigate the influence of smaller \glspl{rootdataset}, as proposed in the original work.
	
	\paragraph{Stateless clients.}
	The clients do not preserve state across \glspl{communicationround}. In combination with Top-$k$ sparsification, this prevents small but consistent gradient updates from accumulating over time.
	
	Introducing client-side state would require \glspl{honestclient} to remain synchronized with the \gls{globalmodel}. While this assumption is realistic for distributed training, it is difficult to justify in federated learning. Alternatively, maintaining the state on the server would reduce the benefits of sparsification and introduce additional privacy concerns.
	
	\paragraph{Privacy budget schedule.}
	The \gls{privacybudget} was allocated uniformly across training rounds. However, recent work by Kiani et al.~\cite{kiani2025differentiallyprivatefederatedlearning} suggests that a time-adaptive allocation yields improved privacy--utility trade-offs.
	
	\subsection{Future Work}
	
	In this section, we derive directions for future research from the open findings and limitations of this work.
	
	\paragraph{Investigate the Interaction of \ac{DP} and FLTrust.}
	Future work should investigate the interaction between \ac{DP} and FLTrust in greater detail, particularly the observed inversion of the influence of the \gls{privacybudget} on model performance, to determine whether this behavior can be reproduced and quantified in more complex experimental settings. Additionally, the evolution of the \glspl{trustscore} should be analyzed more closely to better understand the mechanisms underlying the observed behavior.
	
	\paragraph{Trust Scores as a Regularization Mechanism.}
	The gradual decay of the \glspl{trustscore} once the \gls{globalmodel} has been established suggests that they may capture increasing specialization of the local client models. Future work could therefore investigate whether these \glspl{trustscore} can be exploited as a regularization mechanism to reduce overfitting to individual client datasets.
	
	\paragraph{Broader Experimental Setup.}
	Future work should extend the detailed analysis to CIFAR-10 and more challenging datasets that better reflect practical federated learning applications.
	
	Furthermore, the experimental setup should be expanded to encompass a broader range of aggregation methods, such as Krum, SMEA, and CAF, as well as stronger adversarial scenarios, including the model poisoning attacks proposed by Fang et al.~\cite{fang2020poisoning}. Exploring reparameterization techniques such as differentially private LoRA, as explored by Liu et al..~\cite{liu2025dplora} could further improve the understanding of the trade-offs between efficiency and privacy.
	
	Finally, future work should investigate alternative threat models, including malicious servers and collusion between \glspl{maliciousclient} and the server, as well as non-\ac{IID} data distributions through artificially skewed client datasets. Together, these extensions would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the privacy--robustness--performance trilemma and help determine whether the interactions observed in this work generalize to more realistic federated learning settings.
	
	
	% --------------------------------------------------
	\section{Conclusion}
	\label{sec:conclusion}
	% --------------------------------------------------
	
	Federated learning enables collaborative model training while preserving data locality. However, practical deployments must simultaneously address privacy, Byzantine robustness, and communication efficiency. This work investigated the interactions between \ac{DP-SGD}, FLTrust aggregation, and Top-$k$ sparsification to better understand the resulting privacy–robustness–performance trilemma.
	
	This paper shows that the destructive interaction between differential privacy and FLTrust can be partially mitigated by reducing the number of \glspl{localsgdstep} between \glspl{communicationround}. Furthermore, the combination of both mechanisms exhibits a counterintuitive dependence on the assigned \gls{privacybudget}, highlighting the need for further investigation. In addition, this work provides insights into the temporal evolution of the \glspl{trustscore} used by FLTrust and identifies this behavior as a promising direction for future research.
	
	Future work should validate these findings on larger models, more realistic datasets, stronger attacks, and additional aggregation schemes. The presented visualization and experimental framework provide a basis for navigating the privacy–robustness–performance trilemma in future federated learning systems.
