	\section{Introduction}
	% --------------------------------------------------
	
	We present a systematic empirical study of the Privacy--Robustness--Performance
	trilemma in Federated Learning (FL), examining how simultaneously deployed defences
	interact and compound across all three axes.

	\subsection{Research Questions}
	
	The project is structured around three research questions:
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item \textbf{Isolation:} What is the individual cost of each defense mechanism
		(DP, Byzantine-robust aggregation, gradient compression) in terms of accuracy,
		communication overhead, and attack success rate, and do our results reproduce
		the published baselines?
		\item \textbf{Combination:} How do pairwise and full combinations of the three
		mechanisms interact? Do the costs compound additively, sub-additively, or
		super-additively?
		\item \textbf{Mitigation:} Can simple adjustments --- e.g.\ joint calibration of
		the DP clipping norm and the robust aggregation threshold --- reduce the
		compounding effect without sacrificing formal guarantees?
	\end{enumerate}

	\subsection{Federated Learning}
	
	\placeholder{%
		FedAvg~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}: global model, local SGD, aggregation.
		IID vs.\ non-IID data; communication rounds; client sampling. Image/graphic here?%
	}
	
	Federated Learning is a machine learning framework that consists of a central server and multiple clients. Each client holds an individual dataset for computing their local updates. The central server coordinates the training process and aggregates the updates.
	For each round of training, the central server distributes the current model, waits for the clients to compute the local updates and then collects and aggregates those updates to obtain a global model update that is then shared with the clients to initialize the next round.
	
	The most common algorithm for aggregation is FedAvg~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}, where the gloal update is computed as a weighted average of the individual updates.

	\subsection{Robustness: Byzantine-Tolerant Aggregation}
	
	\subsubsection{Byzantine Fault Model}
	
	\placeholder{%
		Definition of $\eta$-fraction Byzantine adversary; threat capabilities
		(arbitrary gradient manipulation); impossibility results without redundancy.%
	}
	
	A $\eta$-fraction Byzantine adversary means that $\eta$\% of the clients are communicating manipulated gradients to poison the central model, e.g. insert a backdoor, degrade the general performance or enable label flipping attacks.
	
	\subsubsection{Krum}
	
	\placeholder{%
		Blanchard et al.~\cite{blanchard2017krum}: selects update closest to its
		$n-f-2$ neighbours; provably Byzantine-tolerant but accuracy-limited under
		heterogeneity.%
	}
	
	Krum~\cite{blanchard2017krum} was introduced by Blanchard et al. in 2017 to increase robustness to byzantine adversaries by only processing updates that are closest to their XX neighbors, effectively only considering the densest update cluster of size XX. This renders the training process provably Byzantine-tolerant but assumes that honest updates cluster together, which only holds true for similar data distributions across clients. Under data heterogeneity this leads to a decrease in model performance due to the fact that honest updates are discarded if they differ too much from the majority of updates.
	
	\subsubsection{FLTrust}
	
	\placeholder{%
		Cao et al.~\cite{cao2021fltrust}: server holds small root dataset; cosine
		similarity-based trust scores clip client contributions;
		advantages under data heterogeneity.%
	}
	
	FLTrust~\cite{cao2021fltrust} provides protection against malicious updates by holding a small root dataset within the server that is used to compute an update anchor each round. The client updates are then weighted by the cosine similarity to this anchor. The central assumption here is that such a dataset exists and is available to the server, assuming knowledge about the general data distribution across all clients.

	\subsection{The Privacy--Robustness--Performance Trilemma}
	
	\subsubsection{Theoretical Lower Bound}
	
	\placeholder{%
		Allouah et al.~\cite{allouah2023trilemma}: 
		The cross-term $\eta/\epsilon^2$ shows multiplicative, not additive, interaction
		between privacy and robustness.
		SMEA (matches bound, exponential cost) vs.\ CAF (polynomial approximation).%
	}
	
	Allouha et al.~\cite{allouah2023trilemma} showed that any algorithm guaranteeing
	$\epsilon$-local DP and robustness against an $\eta$-fraction Byzantine adversary incurs a mean-estimation error of at least
	$\Omega\!\left(\frac{\eta}{\epsilon^2} + \frac{d}{\epsilon^2 n} + \eta G^2\right)$.
	
	The cross-term $\eta/\epsilon^2$ shows that the cost for privacy and robustness interact multiplicative instead of additive.
